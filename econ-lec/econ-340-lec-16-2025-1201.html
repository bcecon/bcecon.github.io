Climate Risk and Adaptation

What is Climate Risk?

Climate risk is the potential for adverse consequences for human and ecological systems, arising from the interaction of climate-related hazards with the exposure and vulnerability of people, ecosystems, and their biodiversity.

Source: IPCC (2022)

  • Climate risk is not just about how big the storm or flood is.
  • It comes from three ingredients:
    • Climate Hazard ‚Äì the physical climate event (heat waves, droughts, floods, storms)
    • Exposure ‚Äì who/what is in harm‚Äôs way
    • Vulnerability ‚Äì how fragile or resilient society and ecosystems are
In shorthand we can think of:

\[ \boxed{\color{purple}{(\text{Climate Risk})} \;\propto\; \color{#1f77b4}{(\text{Climate Hazard})} \times \color{#17becf}{(\text{Exposure})} \times \color{#E69F00}{(\text{Vulnerability})}} \]

Cost Benefit Analysis under Risk

üåßÔ∏è‚ö° Cost‚ÄìBenefit Analysis under Risk

What is Risk in Economics?

  • Under risk, outcomes are uncertain, but we can assign probabilities to different possible outcomes.
  • Many real-world policy projects have uncertain outcomes:
    • Dams and flood-control projects (risk of catastrophic failure or being overtopped)
    • Urban drainage and stormwater systems (may be underdesigned for future intense rainfall)
    • Drought adaptation in agriculture (e.g., new irrigation systems or drought-tolerant crops may underperform under future climate)
  • A robust Cost‚ÄìBenefit Analysis (CBA) must account for:
    • The range of possible outcomes, and
    • How likely each outcome is.

üìê Incorporating Risk into CBA: Expected Value

Suppose a project can lead to three different outcomes \(x_i\), where \(i = 1,2,3\):

  • Each outcome \(x_i\) has:
    • Probability \(P(x_i)\)
    • Net benefit \(NB(x_i)\)
  • For a single outcome: \[E[NB(x_i)] = P(x_i)\times NB(x_i)\]
  • For all possible outcomes, the expected net benefit of the project is: \[ \begin{aligned} E[NB(X)] &= \sum_{i=1}^3 \bigl[ P(x_i)\times NB(x_i) \bigr]\\ &= P(x_1)\times NB(x_1) \,+\,P(x_2)\times NB(x_2) \,+\,P(x_3)\times NB(x_3) \end{aligned} \]
  • We use this as a summary measure of what the project delivers on average, given the possible outcomes and their probabilities.

Risk Aversion

Imagine you must choose one option:

  • Option A: Receive $50 for sure.

  • Option B: Toss a coin:

    • Heads ‚Üí $100
    • Tails ‚Üí $0

  • The expected value of both options is:

    \[EV = 0.5\times \$100 + 0.5\times \$0 = \$50\]

  • Your risk preference can be categorized as:
    • Risk-averse ‚Üí prefers A (safe $50).
    • Risk-loving ‚Üí prefers B (chance at $100).
    • Risk-neutral ‚Üí indifferent (cares only about EV).

üò¨ Risk Aversion: Why Expected Value Isn‚Äôt Everything

Imagine you must choose one option:

  • Option A: Receive $100 for sure.
  • Option B: \(50\%\) chance of +$300, and \(50\%\) chance of ‚Äì$100.

The expected value of both options:

\[ \begin{aligned} EV(B) &= 0.5\times \$300 + 0.5\times (-\$100) \\ &= \$150 - \$50 = \$100 \end{aligned} \]

  • Expected value treats gains and losses symmetrically, weighted by probabilities.
  • But many people are risk averse:
    • They dislike small chances of large losses, even if the EV looks attractive.
  • In expected value terms, A and B are equal (\(EV = \$100\)),
    but many people still pick A ‚Üí evidence of risk aversion.

üé≤‚ùì Risk vs. Uncertainty (Economist‚Äôs Definitions)

Risk

  • Variability or randomness that can be quantified.
  • We can list possible outcomes and attach probabilities we trust.
  • Example:
    • A \(1\)-in-\(1,000\) chance of a nuclear accident over the plant‚Äôs life.
    • A \(1\)-in-\(5,000\) chance of a major offshore oil spill.
    • Health risks of smoking estimated from statistical data.

Uncertainty

  • Variability or randomness that cannot be reliably quantified.
  • Example: extreme, poorly understood climate outcomes:
    • Massive methane release from thawing permafrost,
    • Large shifts in ocean currents (e.g., weakening of the Gulf Stream).

üö® Uncertainty & the Precautionary Principle

Precautionary Principle & Safe Minimum Standards

When potential impacts are large, irreversible, and deeply uncertain, economists and policymakers often argue for a precautionary principle or a safe minimum standard, rather than relying only on expected value.

  • Precautionary principle:
    Policies should account for uncertainty by taking steps to avoid low-probability but catastrophic events.

  • Safe minimum standard:
    Set environmental policies so as to avoid possible catastrophic consequences.

  • In the dam example:

    • A 1% chance of catastrophic failure barely changes \(E[NB]\),
    • but for people downstream, even a small chance of huge damages may be unacceptable.

Flood Insurance, NFIP, and Moral Hazard

Why Flood Insurance Matters in the U.S.

  • Floods and storms
    • Among the most frequent and costly natural disasters in the U.S.
  • Households and communities
    • Face large, sudden losses.
    • Private insurance markets historically reluctant to offer affordable coverage.

The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP)

  • Created: 1968 (National Flood Insurance Act)
  • Managed by: Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA)
  • Basic idea:
    1. Provide flood insurance where private market is thin.
    2. Reduce disaster costs to taxpayers.
    3. Encourage safer building and land use in risky areas.

NFIP: Main Goals

  1. Provide Flood Insurance
    • Fill the gap when private insurers consider floods ‚Äúuninsurable.‚Äù
  2. Improve Floodplain Management
    • Encourage better land-use and building standards.
  3. Develop Floodplain Maps
    • Identify flood-prone areas and measure risk.

NFIP in Practice: Growing Losses

  • Major hurricanes (Katrina, Sandy, Harvey, Ian, Helene‚Ä¶) account for a large share of losses.
  • NFIP has repeatedly borrowed from the U.S. Treasury and carried large debt.

What Would an ‚ÄúEfficient‚Äù Flood Insurance System Look Like?

  1. Premium Levels
    • On average, premiums should be high enough to cover expected claims and administrative costs.
  2. Risk-Reflective Pricing
    • Each property‚Äôs premium should reflect its individual risk (location, elevation, building type, etc.).
  3. Risk-Reducing Incentives
    • Insurance design should reward mitigation (elevating homes, floodproofing, better zoning).

NFIP Design Problems (1): Pricing and Mapping

  • Underpriced premiums
    • Historically too low relative to expected payouts.
    • NFIP debt reached about $22.5 billion (2025).
  • Outdated flood maps
    • Many maps do not fully reflect current or future climate risk.
    • Some properties face much higher true risk than their maps suggest.

NFIP Design Problems (2): Incentives

  • Limited rewards for mitigation
    • Discounts exist but are often small or not well understood.
  • Repeated losses
    • Some properties flood again and again, with NFIP paying each time.
    • Example: One home flooded 40 times, receiving $428,379 in payments.
  • Grandfathering
    • Older policies may keep lower rates even as risk rises.
    • Weakens the ‚Äúprice signal‚Äù that should encourage safer locations and designs.

From NFIP Problems to Economic Incentives

Key question:

When we insure people against flood losses, do we unintentionally encourage them to take more risk?

  • Cheap or subsidized insurance can:
    • Make it more attractive to stay in high-risk areas.
    • Make rebuilding in the same risky spot more likely.
  • This brings us to the central concept: moral hazard.

Moral Hazard: Basic Idea

  • Definition:
    Moral hazard occurs when having insurance causes people to change their behavior, taking more risk because they do not bear the full cost of bad outcomes.
  • Simple example (car insurance):
    • With full coverage, a driver might:
      • Drive a bit faster.
      • Be less careful about where they park.
    • They still suffer some hassle‚Äîbut most of the financial loss is covered.

Moral Hazard in the NFIP

  • NFIP offers flood insurance, often at subsidized or below-market rates.
  • For some owners, this can change behavior:
    • More likely to rebuild in the same flood-prone area.
    • Less incentive to invest in elevation, floodproofing, or relocation.
  • Result:
    • Higher exposure of people and property to future floods.
    • Repeated claims and rising costs for NFIP and taxpayers.

How Underpriced Insurance Changes Incentives

Without insurance:

  • Risky location ‚áí High expected damage cost borne by homeowner.
  • Strong incentive to avoid or mitigate risk.

With underpriced insurance:

  • Risky location ‚áí Many costs shifted to NFIP/taxpayers.
  • Homeowner‚Äôs out-of-pocket cost is smaller, so:
    • Risky locations may look ‚Äúcheap‚Äù to them.
    • Safer locations or mitigation efforts may look less attractive.

Addressing Moral Hazard in NFIP (Property Level)

  1. Premium Adjustments
    • Move toward risk-based premiums so that riskier properties pay more.
  2. Mitigation Incentives
    • Offer premium discounts for:
      • Elevating buildings
      • Floodproofing
      • Moving mechanical systems above flood level
  3. Restrictions on Rebuilding
    • Limit coverage or require mitigation for properties with repeated claims.

Addressing Moral Hazard in NFIP (Community Level)

  1. Community Rating System (CRS)
    • NFIP scores communities based on their flood risk management efforts.
    • Better scores ‚áí premium discounts for all policyholders in that community.
    • Gives local governments incentives to:
      • Improve zoning and building codes.
      • Invest in drainage, levees, and other protective measures.

Recent NFIP Reforms

  • Biggert‚ÄìWaters Act (2012)
    • Attempted to move NFIP toward financial soundness and risk-based premiums.
  • Homeowner Flood Insurance Affordability Act (2014)
    • Slowed some premium increases for affordability reasons.
  • Risk Rating 2.0 (2021)
    • New pricing system that aims to reflect each property‚Äôs unique risk more accurately.
  • Disaster Recovery Reform Act (2018)
    • Increased funding for pre-disaster mitigation.

A Puzzle: Moral Hazard vs. Underinsurance

  • Moral hazard suggests too much risk-taking because people are insured.
  • But in reality:
    • Only about 49% of residents in high-risk flood zones purchase insurance.
  • Behavioral biases help explain underinsurance:
    • Myopia: focus on the premium today, not potential loss.
    • Amnesia: forget past disasters.
    • Optimism: ‚ÄúIt won‚Äôt happen to me.‚Äù
    • Inertia, simplification, herding.

Behavioral Biases and Flood Preparedness

  • Myopia: up-front premium feels large; future loss feels distant.
  • Amnesia: if a big flood hasn‚Äôt happened recently, risk feels low.
  • Optimism: underestimate probability and damage.
  • Inertia: delay buying or renewing insurance.
  • Simplification: ignore complex flood maps and probabilities.
  • Herding: ‚ÄúNone of my neighbors have insurance, so I don‚Äôt need it.‚Äù

Policy Implications

  • Design challenge:
    • Provide financial protection and support rebuilding.
    • Avoid encouraging risky behavior (moral hazard).
    • Overcome behavioral biases that lead to underinsurance.
  • Policy tools:
    • Risk-based premiums with targeted subsidies for low-income households.
    • Stronger building codes, zoning, and buyout programs in severe-risk areas.
    • Better risk communication and default options (e.g., automatic coverage with opt-out).
