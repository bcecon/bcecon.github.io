üí° Value of Statistical Life

‚öñÔ∏è How Should Society Value Life-Saving Policies?

üåç Policy Context

Public policies on air quality, drinking water, climate change, and safety standards directly affect mortality risks ‚Äî for example, by reducing deaths from pollution exposure, unsafe water, or extreme weather events.

  • Although human life cannot be priced, resources are limited ‚Äî forcing society to make trade-offs in efforts to reduce risks.

  • Significance: Estimating the value of human life is crucial for allocating limited resources across programs that aim to save lives and reduce illness.

  • Core Dilemma: Because life is priceless yet resources are scarce, policymakers must determine how much to spend for incremental reductions in mortality risk.

How much should society be willing to pay for small reductions in the risk of death?

üßÆ Economic Approach to Valuing Life

  • Focus: Economists value reductions in mortality risk, not life itself.
  • Concept: The Value of a Statistical Life (VSL) represents the aggregate willingness to pay (WTP) for small reductions in mortality risk that sum to ‚Äúone statistical life‚Äù saved.
  • Calculation:
    • Based on the marginal rate of substitution between money and mortality risk.
    • Reflects individuals‚Äô trade-offs between income and risk reduction.

üß© Calculating VSL

Scenario:
Police officers receive an extra pay of $700 per year for an excess fatality risk of 1/10,000 annually.

What is the implied VSL?

  1. Extra Pay = $700 per year
  2. Change in Mortality Risk = 1/10,000
  3. \[ \text{VSL} = \frac{\text{Extra WTP}}{\text{Change in Mortality Risk}} = \frac{700}{1/10{,}000} = 700 \times 10{,}000 = \$7{,}000{,}000 \]

Result: The Value of Statistical Life is $7 million.

  • Interpretation: 10,000 officers collectively value the prevention of ‚Äúone statistical death‚Äù at $7 million.

üßÆ Calculating VSL for a Policy Scenario

A new safety policy lowers the annual risk of death from 1 in 100,000 to 1 in 150,000 for 1 million people.

  • Expected deaths before policy: 10
  • Expected deaths after policy: 6.67
  • Lives saved: ‚âà 3.33
  • Each person‚Äôs WTP: $5 ‚Üí Total WTP = $5 million

Question: What is the implied Value of a Statistical Life (VSL)?

\[ \text{VSL} = \frac{\text{Total WTP}}{\text{Lives Saved}} \]

or

\[ \text{VSL} = \frac{\text{Individual WTP}}{\text{Change in Risk}} \]

üí° Result: The implied Value of a Statistical Life (VSL) is approximately $1.5 million.

üßÆ Calculating VSL: Alternative Scenario

A new vehicle safety feature reduces the annual risk of a fatal accident by 1 in 100,000 (0.001%).

If each individual in a population of 100,000 is willing to pay $100 for this risk reduction, what is the implied Value of a Statistical Life (VSL)?

‚öñÔ∏è Is Valuing Human Life Immoral?

  • Moral Objections:
    • Critics contend that placing a monetary value on human life is inherently immoral.
    • Doing so may undermine the absolute right to live free from pollution-induced harm.
    • Regulations that permit some deaths for the sake of cost efficiency raise serious ethical concerns.

üõ†Ô∏è Defense of Benefit‚ÄìCost Analysis

  • Resource Reality: When resources are limited, ignoring the benefits of lifesaving measures would itself be unethical.
  • Inevitable Trade-offs: Completely eliminating pollution or risk is technically impossible and economically unsustainable.
  • Everyday Precedent: Individuals routinely balance risk and cost (e.g., driving speed, diet, medical decisions).
  • Democratic Fairness: Transparent benefit‚Äìcost analysis ensures that public preferences and opportunity costs are openly considered.

üí° While imperfect, benefit‚Äìcost analysis using the VSL provides a transparent and pragmatic framework for evaluating how policies improve overall welfare

  • Income elasticity of VSL measures how the VSL changes in response to income growth ‚Äî that is, the percentage change in VSL for a 1% change in income.
  • The estimated elasticity tends to be higher in lower-income countries, reflecting greater sensitivity of WTP to income changes.
  • Analysts use these estimates to adjust VSL values across countries or over time as income levels change.
  • Typical ranges: >1.0 in lower-income contexts, and 0.4‚Äì0.6 for U.S. and other high-income countries.
    • When income falls, WTP tends to drop sharply (because basic needs dominate spending).

üá∫üá∏Ô∏è Historical Shifts in U.S. Agency VSLs

  • George W. Bush administration: EPA‚Äôs VSL fell to $6.8 million.
  • Obama administration:
    • EPA raised its VSL to $9.1 million (2010) and $9.7 million (2013).
    • FDA increased its VSL from $5 million (2008) ‚Üí $7.9 million (2010) ‚Üí $9.3 million (2015).
    • DOT adopted stronger safety rules (e.g., car roof standards) once higher VSLs justified their cost.
  • Trump and Biden administrations: VSL values remained largely unchanged, adjusted only for inflation and income growth.
    • $10.7 million (2022 dollars)

‚öôÔ∏è Applications of the VSL

  • Environmental Regulation ‚Äî U.S. Clean Air Act (1990‚Äì2020):
    • The EPA estimates the Act has prevented over 230,000 premature deaths annually.
    • About 85% of total benefits stem from mortality risk reductions valued using the VSL.
    • The annual benefits (~$2 trillion) outweigh costs by a factor of over 30 to 1.
  • Climate Change Policy:
    • The VSL is used to value mortality from heat-related deaths (~9,700 per year in the U.S.), which informs estimates of the SCC.
  • Public Health Decisions:
    • Used to assess trade-offs between economic costs and lives saved, such as in safety standards, air quality, or pandemic policies.

ü¶† Case Study: COVID-19 and the Value of Life

  • Economic Impact:
    • Social distancing generated an estimated $8 trillion in avoided mortality in the U.S.
    • Total U.S. mortality costs ‚âà $1.4 trillion (first half of 2020).
    • Global mortality costs ‚âà $3.5 trillion by mid-2020.
  • Historical Insight:
    • Cities with stronger 1918 flu interventions recovered faster economically, showing that saving lives and economic resilience can go hand in hand.
  • Key Takeaway:
    • Public health measures can simultaneously improve health and economic outcomes.
    • The supposed ‚Äúlives vs. economy‚Äù trade-off is largely misleading.

ü™∂ Vultures in India ‚Äî Valuing Keystone Species

  • Vultures are keystone scavengers whose carcass removal prevents disease transmission and supports ecosystem health.
  • Their value reflects the economic benefits of this ecological role ‚Äî captured through avoided mortality, reduced disease, and sustained productivity.
  • Crisis:
    Populations collapsed in the 1990s due to diclofenac, a veterinary anti-inflammatory drug toxic to vultures.
  • Consequence:
    Decomposition slowed, leading to more rotting carcasses and disease transmission.

üìâ Mortality Impacts of Vulture Decline

  • Between 2000‚Äì2005, the loss of vultures caused roughly 100,000 additional human deaths per year in India.
  • Estimated mortality damages: $69.4 billion per year, based on Indian VSL estimates.
