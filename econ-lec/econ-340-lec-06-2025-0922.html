Common Property, Open Access, and Property Rights

Four Property-Right Regimes: The Landscape

  • Private property is not the only way to define entitlements to resource use. Consider:
    • State-property regimes — government owns and controls access and rules.
    • Common-property regimes — a specified group co-owns and co-manages the resource.
    • Res nullius / Open access regimes — no owner, no authority to exclude or set rules.
  • Each regime creates distinct incentives for harvest, investment, monitoring, and conservation.

State Property — Design & Pitfalls

  • Present to some degree in virtually all countries (e.g., parks, wilderness preserves).
  • Upside: Can pursue social objectives (biodiversity, equity) with legal authority to exclude/enforce.
  • Risk: Principal–agent problems—bureaucratic incentives may diverge from society’s interests (e.g., budget maximization, political influence, information gaps).
    • Principal–agent problems: the conflict that arises when one party (the principal) delegates authority to another party (the agent) to act on their behalf, and the agent’s interests do not align with the principal’s.
  • Performance hinges on: credible science, monitoring/enforcement capacity, accountability.

Common Property — When It Works (and When It Doesn’t)

  • Definition: Shared resources managed in common by a defined group; rights may be formal (law) or informal (custom).
  • Efficiency & sustainability depend on rule quality (fit to ecology), compliance, conflict resolution, and trust.
  • Reality check: Many successes exist—but breakdowns are common when cohesion or enforcement erodes.

Common Property — When It Works (and When It Doesn’t)

Case: Swiss Alpine Meadows (Common Property Success)

  • Grazing treated as common property for centuries.
  • User associations set stocking rules; stable membership fostered reciprocity and trust.
  • Outcome: Overgrazing discouraged; compliance high; sustained yields.

Case: Mawelle, Sri Lanka (Governance Breakdown)

  • Initially: Rotating rights over fishing spots/times ensured equity and protected stocks.
  • Later: Population growth + outsiders eroded cohesion; rules became unenforceable.
  • Outcome: Overexploitation and lower incomes.
  • Success ingredients: reciprocity, stable membership, transparent rules, graduated sanctions, local monitoring.

Rivalry & Excludability

Rival? Does one user’s harvest reduce what’s left for others?

Excludable? Can users who haven’t paid be kept out?

Open Access (Res nullius) — The Core Problem

  • No one can exclude ⇒ access is nonexcludable; the stock is rival (one user’s harvest reduces others’).
  • Result: First-come, first-served use; individuals ignore the stock externality on others and the future.
  • Popular label: “Tragedy of the open-access commons”—overuse and rent dissipation as scarcity grows.

Note

  • Key distinction: Open access ⟂ Common property.

  • Open access = common property without effective rules or governance.

  • All open-access resources are common-pool resources (CPR), but not all common-pool resources are open access.

American Bison as a Common-Pool Example

Northern and Southern Herds, circa 1865

  • Early U.S. period: Abundant bison; unrestricted hunting did not impose noticeable scarcity costs.

  • As demand/technology rose: Scarcity emerged; each hunter’s effort reduced others’ catch per unit effort.

  • Open access + scarcity ⇒ escalating effort, falling stocks, near-extinction.

Total Framework (Top Panel)

  • Profits \(\Pi(E) = TR(E) - TC(E)\).
  • Total Revenue \(TR(E) = P \times H(E)\) with price \(P\) assumed constant.
  • Total Cost \(TC(E)\): increases with effort \(E\) (linear if marginal cost \(c\) is constant).
  • Efficient effort \(E^{*}\): maximizes the vertical gap between TR and TC.

Marginal Framework (Bottom Panel)

  • Marginal Revenue \(MR(E) = \frac{dTR}{dE}\) declines as stock falls with effort.
  • Marginal Cost \(MC(E)\) (e.g., constant \(c\)).
  • Efficiency: \(MR(E^{*}) = MC(E^{*})\).
  • Two panels are equivalent representations of the same optimum.

Locating the Efficient Point \(E^{*}\)

  • In totals: \(E^{*}\) maximizes TR − TC.
  • In margins: \(MR = MC\).
  • Interpretation: At \(E^{*}\), the last unit of effort adds as much revenue as it costs.

Open-Access Outcome \(E_{OA}\): Why It’s Too High

  • With free entry, users expand effort until profit per user = 0.
  • Condition: Average Revenue \(AR(E_{OA}) = MC\) (with constant \(c\)).
  • Because \(AR > MR\) when TR shows diminishing returns, \(E_{OA} > E^{*}\) and rents are dissipated.

Why Open Access Dissipates Rents

  • No exclusion ⇒ no appropriation of surplus.
  • Each user ignores the stock externality imposed on others (and on the future).
  • Effort expands until normal profit (per-user profit \(=0\))—total economic rent falls to zero.
  • Formally (constant \(c\)): \(AR(E_{OA}) = MC = c\), while \(E_{OA} > E^{*}\) and \(TS(E_{OA}) < TS(E^{*})\).

Comparative Incentives

  • Private: Owner internalizes returns (intertemporal), incentivizing conservation subject to discounting/externalities.
  • State: Can set socially oriented rules; success depends on capacity, information, and accountability.
  • Common: Effective when rules fit local conditions, participation is broad, and monitoring/sanctions are credible.
  • Open Access: Systematically overshoots efficient effort; rent dissipation and higher collapse risk as price/technology rise.

Policy Levers to Realign Incentives

  • Define/strengthen rights: Privatization or robust common-property institutions (where feasible and equitable).
  • Regulate access/harvest: Licenses, quotas, ITQs (individual transferable quotas), seasonal/gear limits; invest in monitoring & enforcement.
  • Price instruments: Access/landing fees to internalize stock externalities; earmark revenues for stewardship/transition.
  • Capacity building: Support user associations, conflict resolution, and nested local–state governance.

Russia’s “Peanut Hole”

  • Location: A high seas area in the center of the Sea of Okhotsk, surrounded by Russia’s EEZ (Exclusive Economic Zone), which generally extends 200 nautical miles from a coastal state’s baselines.
  • Significance:
    • Previously a global commons outside Russia’s jurisdiction.
    • Known as the “Peanut Hole” due to its unique shape.
  • Challenges:
    • Overfishing by international fleets posed threats to fish stocks.
    • Difficulty in coordinating sustainable management of resources in this area.
  • Resolution: In 2014, Russia gained control of the “Peanut Hole” under UN Convention on LOS (Law of the Sea) provisions, integrating it into its EEZ.

Community-Devised Governance of the Commons

  • Alternative to private ownership or top-down regulation: users can craft agreements to avoid the tragedy of the commons.
  • Elinor Ostrom (Nobel 2009): many communities succeed via self-organization without privatization or heavy state control.
  • Local users hold context-specific knowledge (ecology, seasons, customs) often missing from centralized rule setting (fees, harvest limits).
  • Users recognize that short-run individual gains can cause long-run ecological/economic collapse, so they adopt precautionary rules.

Community-Devised Governance of the Commons

Conditions for effective local cooperation

  • Broad participation in rulemaking by most users.
  • Accountable monitoring conducted by or answerable to users, with regular checks of conditions and compliance.
  • Low-cost, responsive conflict resolution mechanisms.
  • Rules tailored to local ecology, culture, and technology.
  • Graduated sanctions that escalate with repeated violations.

Nested Governance & Policy Levers

  • Ostrom’s approach can complement government for large-scale commons via nested institutions.
  • Example: a state/federal agency administers/enforces an ITQ program; local fishers co-design allocation rules and handle day-to-day disputes.
  • Effective governance is participatory, combining local (including Indigenous) knowledge, history, and culture with broader legal/scientific capacity.
  • At national/global scales, government involvement is essential, while policy still respects local variation and incorporates community voices.
