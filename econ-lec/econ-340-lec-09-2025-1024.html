The Discount Rate

Why Discount the Future?

  • In most environmental policies, costs/benefits often occur at different times
  • Converting everything to real (inflation-adjusted) dollars handles inflation‚Äîbut not time preference
  • People (and societies) often prefer benefits now (investment opportunity, uncertainty, impatience)
  • Therefore we apply discounting to compare future and present values on a common basis

Note

  • Idea: $100 today can be worth the same as $200 ten years from now at an appropriate discount rate.

Present Value (PV) Formula

Let \(X_t\) be a cost/benefit received in \(t\) years and \(r\) the discount rate:

\[ \text{PV}(X_t) \;=\; \frac{X_t}{(1+r)^{\,t}} \]

  • Further in time (\(t\uparrow\)) ‚Üí PV falls
  • Higher discount rate (\(r\uparrow\)) ‚Üí PV falls

Tip

  • Examples (for \(X_t = \$100\))
    • \(r=3\%, t=10 \Rightarrow \text{PV}=\$74.41\)
    • \(r=7\%, t=10 \Rightarrow \text{PV}=\$50.83\)

How Fast Does PV Decline?

  • High \(r\) greatly devalues distant impacts
    • \(r=7\%, t=50\Rightarrow \text{PV}=\$3.39\) for $100
    • \(r=10\%, t=50\Rightarrow \text{PV}=\$0.85\)
  • Small changes in \(r\) matter a lot over long horizons
    • After 100 years, PV at 1% is about seven times greater than the PV at 3%

üí° Quick Detour: The Rule of 72

A simple way to estimate how long it takes for a value to double given a fixed annual growth (or interest) rate.

\[ \text{Years to Double} \approx \frac{72}{r} \]

where \(r\) is the annual percentage rate (not in decimal form).

  • The higher the rate of return, the faster the doubling.
  • Works well for rates between 2% and 15%.
  • Useful for understanding economic/financial growth, inflation, or discounting.
    • Example: If the economy grows at 3% per year, its size doubles in about 24 years.

Choosing the Discount Rate: Two Approaches

Why Discount the Future?

The discount rate answers a fundamental question:

At what rate should society discount future benefits so that sacrificing one unit of consumption today is fairly balanced by gains to future well-being?

  • This rate reflects how we value the future relative to the present, and it can be determined in two main ways:
    • Through observed market outcomes ‚Üí the market-based (descriptive) approach
    • Through ethical principles about intergenerational welfare ‚Üí the social (prescriptive) approach

Social Discount Rate (SDR)

\[ \text{SDR} = \rho + \eta \cdot g \]

  • \(\rho\): pure rate of time preference ‚Äî captures impatience or preference for present welfare over future welfare
  • \(g\): growth rate of real consumption ‚Äî reflects how average living standards or income per person grow over time
  • \(\eta\): elasticity of marginal utility of consumption ‚Äî measures how quickly the extra satisfaction (utility) from consumption declines as people become richer
  • Welfare growth effect: \(\eta\) and \(g\) together determine how the growth in income (\(g\)) translates into growth in social welfare.
    • \(g\) drives the rise in consumption
    • \(\eta\) adjusts how much extra happiness that additional consumption brings.

SDR ‚Äî Linking \(\eta\), Diminishing Utility, and Fairness

  • Higher \(\eta\) ‚Üí Faster Diminishing Marginal Utility
    • The extra satisfaction from each additional dollar drops faster as consumption rises.
  • Higher \(\eta\) ‚Üí Stronger Concern for Equality
    • Since extra consumption benefits the poor more than the rich, society with a higher \(\eta\) places more weight on poorer or earlier generations.

Note

The Opportunity Cost of Capital is the return forgone when spending $1 today on a project, instead of investing that same $1 in the best available alternative (for example, in private capital markets or government bonds).

  • Set \(\text{SDR} \approx\) low-risk market return (e.g., government bonds or average real return on capital)
  • Logic: Funds spent today could have been invested ‚Äî the opportunity cost of capital determines the appropriate \(\text{SDR}\)
  • Idea: Society‚Äôs time preference between present and future consumption is revealed through saving, investment, and consumption decisions, as reflected in observed market returns and long-run economic growth.
  • Caveat: Market rates change over time
    • Very low in the 2020s; very high in the early 1980s

1Ô∏è‚É£ Market-Based (Descriptive) Approach

‚ÄúHow do markets actually value the future?‚Äù

  • Sets parameters based on observed market behavior, not ethical judgments
    • \(\rho\): higher (‚âà 1‚Äì2%) ‚Äî reflects society‚Äôs revealed time preference in saving and investment decisions
    • \(\eta\) (Higher, ‚âà 1.5) ‚Äî means society places greater weight on poorer or earlier generations
    • \(g\): assumes stronger long-run growth (e.g., 2%), consistent with historical productivity trends
  • Result: Higher SDR (‚âà 4‚Äì5%) ‚Üí less weight on distant future impacts and supports relatively gradual, cost-efficient mitigation og GHG

2Ô∏è‚É£ Ethical (Prescriptive) Approach

‚ÄúWhat should be the fair tradeoff between present and future welfare?‚Äù

  • Parameters are chosen based on ethical reasoning about intergenerational fairness, rather than market behavior
    • \(\rho\): very low (‚âà 0‚Äì0.1%) ‚Üí future welfare is valued almost equally to present welfare
    • \(\eta\): low (‚âà 1) ‚Üí society gives less additional value to income gains for richer future generations
    • \(g\): assumes modest long-run growth (‚âà 1‚Äì1.5%), consistent with sustainability goals
  • Result: Low Social Discount Rate (SDR) (‚âà 1‚Äì2%) ‚Üí places greater weight on future generations and stronger justification for early climate action

üìä Policy Relevance & Ethics

  • 2018 Survey of Economists:
    • Mean = 2.3%, Median = 2.0% (real SDR for intergenerational global projects)
    • 68% of economists recommend a rate between 1‚Äì3%
    • Trend: Steady shift toward lower discount rates since 2001, reflecting greater concern for long-term welfare
  • High \(r\) ‚Üí prioritizes the present, discounting future impacts heavily
  • Low \(r\) ‚Üí gives greater weight to future generations
  • In environmental cost‚Äìbenefit analysis (e.g., climate policy):
    • Costs occur now, benefits arise later ‚Üí a lower \(r\) supports stronger environmental protection
  • There is no single ‚Äúcorrect‚Äù rate ‚Äî analysts should be transparent, justify their assumptions, and test results through sensitivity analysis.

Cost‚ÄìBenefit Analysis

What Is Cost‚ÄìBenefit Analysis (CBA)?

CBA evaluates whether the present value of benefits exceeds the present value of costs for a project or policy.

  • All costs and benefits are expressed in today‚Äôs dollars using a discount rate (\(r\))
  • Enables meaningful comparison of outcomes occurring at different times
  • Helps allocate scarce resources efficiently across competing alternatives

CBA in Government Decision-Making

Governments use CBA to decide:

  • Which projects should be funded under budget constraints
  • How to balance economic, health, and environmental goals
  • Whether policy benefits justify the present value of implementation costs

Example: Regulating Ozone Pollution

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) must decide:
Should the national standard for ground-level ozone be tightened?

  • Ozone affects:
    • Human health (asthma, emphysema)
    • Crop and vegetation damage
  • Stricter standards ‚Üí higher abatement costs now, health benefits later

Steps in Conducting a PV-Based CBA

  1. List all costs and benefits over time
  2. Quantify each stream in monetary terms
  3. Discount each future value to its present value using rate \(r\)
  4. Sum PVs of costs and benefits:

\[ PV_B = \sum_t \frac{B_t}{(1 + r)^t}, \qquad PV_C = \sum_t \frac{C_t}{(1 + r)^t} \]

  1. Compute PV Net Benefits and PV Benefit‚ÄìCost Ratio
  2. Compare across policy alternatives, including a baseline (no action)

üßÆ Decision Criteria (Using Present Value)

1Ô∏è‚É£ PV of Net Benefits

\[ PV_{NB} = PV_B - PV_C \]

  • \(PV_{NB}\) (Present Value of Net Benefits) measures total welfare change in today‚Äôs dollars.
    • Adds up all future benefits (\(B_t\)) and costs (\(C_t\)), converted into present value using the discount rate (\(r\)).
  • If \(PV_{NB} > 0\), benefits exceed costs ‚Üí policy improves total welfare and is worth undertaking.

2Ô∏è‚É£ PV Benefit‚ÄìCost Ratio

\[ PV_{B/C} = \frac{PV_B}{PV_C} \]

  • Compares the relative size of discounted benefits and costs.
  • If \(PV_{B/C} > 1\), benefits outweigh costs in present-value terms.
  • Useful for comparing multiple projects when budgets are limited.

Opportunity Cost and Dynamic Efficiency

Even if \(PV_{NB} > 0\):

  • Another policy might yield a higher \(PV_{NB}\)
  • Always consider the opportunity cost of choosing one project over another
  • Economics seeks to maximize \(PV_{NB}\) ‚Äî the condition for dynamic efficiency
    • Dynamic efficiency is achieved when the allocation of resources over time maximizes the present value of net benefits to society.
  • Dynamic efficiency helps decide how much to use today and how much to save or invest for the future (e.g., in renewable energy, conservation, or infrastructure), since resources spent now can‚Äôt be used later.

Distributional and Ethical Issues ‚öñÔ∏è

  • CBA aggregates all PV benefits and costs across society
    ‚Üí ignores who gains and who bears the costs
  • Example:
    • PV benefits ‚Üí wealthier households (longer lifespan, better access)
    • PV costs ‚Üí lower-income communities (energy price hikes)
  • Even with positive \(PV_{NB}\), may still be inequitable
