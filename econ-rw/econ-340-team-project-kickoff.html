🌍 Research Kick-off — Climate Change and the Individual

Project Guideline

Author
Affiliation

Byeong-Hak Choe

SUNY Geneseo

Published

October 22, 2025

🎯 Purpose

The Research Kick-off Report is your team’s preliminary version of the final research proposal.
It helps you move from ideas to economic questions grounded in the theme:

“Climate Change and the Individual.”

You’ll connect what we’ve learned — market failures, discounting, SCC, and risk — to a behavioral or policy question about individual decision-making.

🧩 Connecting Theory to Practice

We’ve explored the sources of market failure in environmental and energy economics:

  • Negative and positive externalities
  • Public goods and common-pool resources
  • Information problems and principal-agent problems
  • Behavioral anomalies

Now we focus on the economics of climate change, where individuals’ actions aggregate into global consequences.
Key analytical tools we will further discuss include:

  • Dynamic efficiency and discounting — balancing present and future welfare
  • Strategic interaction and game theory — analyzing cooperation and incentives in international environmental agreements (IEAs)
  • Risk and uncertainty — evaluating climate policy under imperfect information and probabilistic outcomes

🧾 Research Kick-off Report — Structure

Your Research Kick-off Report (2–3 pages) is a concise proposal that outlines what you plan to study, why it matters economically, and how you might study it.

It should include the following sections:

  1. Title & Connection to Theme
    • Provide a clear, descriptive title.
    • Explain how your topic fits within “Climate Change and the Individual.”
  2. Research Question & Motivation
    • State your central research question clearly and precisely.
    • Explain why this question is economically meaningful or policy relevant.
    • Highlight the underlying economic problem (e.g., externality, behavioral bias, or coordination failure).
  3. Conceptual Framework
    • Identify the key economic mechanism or theoretical model that frames your question.
    • Specify whether it involves market failure, behavioral economics, or strategic interaction (e.g., game theory).
    • Mention any relevant analytical tools (e.g., discounting, dynamic efficiency, SCC, or risk and uncertainty).
  4. Proposed Data or Methodology
    • Describe how you would analyze your question — using surveys, experiments, secondary data, or theoretical modeling.
    • Clarify the type of evidence or reasoning you would use to answer the question.
  5. Expected Insights or Policy Implications
    • Summarize what kind of findings or implications you anticipate.
    • Discuss how your analysis might inform individual behavior, firm decisions, or public policy.
Note

💡 Think of this report as your research blueprint, not a finished product.
Your goal is to define a focused, economically grounded question and a feasible approach — not to provide final results.

🧠 Brainstorming Examples

Theme Example Research Question Economic Concept Policy Levers
Energy Behavior Why do households underinvest in energy-efficient appliances? Myopia / bounded rationality 💰 Incentives – Align financial signals with efficient choices
Consumption Do “low-carbon” labels affect purchasing choices? Information asymmetry 🧠 Information – Make efficiency visible, comparable, and easy to act on
Insurance & Risk How does climate risk perception affect home insurance demand? Risk aversion / misperception 💰 Incentives – Price risk accurately through insurance and disclosure

💡 Policy Strategy Overview

Category Description
💰 Incentives Align financial signals with efficient, socially optimal choices (e.g., taxes, rebates, insurance premiums).
🧠 Information Make efficiency and risk visible, comparable, and actionable through labels, nudges, or transparency.
🏛 Institutions Set rules and structures (like building codes, subsidies, or property rights) that correct split incentives.
🤝 Social Norms Harness peer effects, moral framing, and social signaling to encourage pro-environmental behavior.

Effective climate policy often combines multiple levers — e.g., tax + label + norm feedback — to align private and social incentives.

🧮 From Topic to Research Question

  1. Start with a behavior
    • e.g., buying EVs, using AC, eating meat, recycling.
  2. Add an economic mechanism
    • incentives, information, externalities, or risk perception.
  3. Make it measurable
    • Can you collect or use data to estimate a relationship or elasticity?

Example transformations:

  • “EV adoption” → “Do upfront costs and discounting deter adoption of electric vehicles?”
  • “Meat consumption” → “How do carbon labels influence dietary choices?”

⚖️ Economic Focus

A strong proposal should:

  • Ask a why or how question about incentives, risk, or preferences
  • Relate to efficiency, welfare, or policy
  • Identify a market failure or behavioral bias
  • Use at least one of the course’s main frameworks:
    • Externalities
    • Public goods
    • Discounting
    • Social cost of carbon (SCC)
    • Risk and uncertainty

🧭 Research Idea Logic

Your project doesn’t need to include advanced modeling or econometrics — but it must show clear economic reasoning and, ideally, connect to real-world data.

Think like an economist: focus on incentives, information, and trade-offs, and support your reasoning with evidence, even if it’s simple or descriptive.

Type Purpose Example Question
Descriptive Identify or illustrate a real-world pattern “Who installs rooftop solar panels?”
Explanatory (Positive) Explain why something happens using theory or reasoning “Why do some households adopt solar while others don’t?”
Conceptual / Mechanism-Based Use an economic concept to describe how incentives or information shape behavior “How could rebates or information programs increase solar adoption?”
Normative / Policy-Oriented Explore what should happen to improve welfare or efficiency “Should carbon rebates target renters or homeowners?”

💾 Using Data Effectively

You are encouraged to:

  • Look for public datasets related to your topic (energy use, weather, attitudes, emissions, adaptation behavior, etc.).
  • Use simple summaries — such as tables, graphs, or averages — to describe your findings.
    • Formal regression analysis is optional, and recommended only if you feel comfortable and have the time to do it carefully.
    • 💬 Ask Prof. Choe for help with any data work! Guidance is available for data search, cleaning, visualization, and statistical modeling.
  • Combine qualitative insights (e.g., survey reports, interviews, case studies) with quantitative evidence where possible.
  • Always cite your data source clearly — for example: EPA, NOAA, World Bank, Our World in Data, U.S. Census, or academic studies.

“You don’t need complex statistics to do good economics — but you do need data and reasoning to support your argument.”

✅ Checklist

Before Submitting

  • Topic fits the theme Climate Change and the Individual
  • Clear and concise research question
  • Identifies economic mechanism or theory
  • Specifies data/method idea
  • Highlights potential findings or implications
  • 2–3 pages, professional tone, one submission per team
